The climate is leaving its orbit.

A century and a half of global temperature — through the present — drawn not as a timeline but as a phase portrait: where the climate is (horizontal) against how fast it is moving (vertical). Time is implicit; dynamics are explicit. For ninety years the system circled a fixed point, the signature of a stable attractor. Around 1970 the trajectory broke out and has not looked back.

Tipping has a shape.

A tipping element like the Atlantic overturning circulation (AMOC) behaves like a ball resting in a potential well. Forcing — freshwater from Greenland melt — tilts the landscape. The well the ball sits in grows shallower long before it disappears; when it vanishes at the fold, the ball rolls to the other state. Drag the forcing past the fold, then try to drag it back: the return trip is not the same as the trip out. That asymmetry is hysteresis, and it is why a collapsed circulation would not simply restart.

The fall announces itself.

As a well shallows, recovery from small shocks slows down — so year-to-year fluctuations grow larger and more persistent. Rising variance and rising lag-1 autocorrelation are the standard early-warning statistics of critical slowing down. Below, both are computed live in your browser from the instrumental record (31-year sliding window on detrended residuals). These are the same instruments used to argue the AMOC is destabilizing (Boers 2021; Ditlevsen & Ditlevsen 2023) — here pointed at the global record as a demonstration of method.

Where this goes as science.

The portrait above is a demonstration. The research program behind it is to make the dynamical-systems view operational — measured continuously, on open data, for every major tipping element.

01 · observatory

A live tipping-point observatory

Compute early-warning indicators continuously from open feeds — RAPID array overturning transport, subpolar-gyre SST, GRACE ice mass, Amazon VOD — with honest uncertainty, published as a versioned, citable data product rather than a one-off paper figure.

02 · landscape

Potentials reconstructed from data

The well above is a cartoon; the real one can be estimated. Langevin/Fokker–Planck reconstruction turns observed fluctuations into an empirical potential landscape — letting us watch the well flatten in measurements instead of assuming its shape.

03 · cascade

Coupled tipping cascades

Tipping elements interact: Greenland melt freshens the Atlantic; an AMOC slowdown shifts monsoons and Amazon rainfall. A probabilistic cascade graph over the elements — with early-warning state as node input — connects local warnings into a global risk picture.
