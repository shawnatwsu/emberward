Tipping points have a shape.

The Atlantic circulation (AMOC) — the current that keeps Europe warm — behaves like a ball in a well. Meltwater tilts the landscape. Drag the forcing past the fold and the well holding the ball simply vanishes. Now try to drag it back: the ball doesn't come back. That one-way door is why scientists lose sleep over this current.

The fall announces itself.

Before a well vanishes, it gets shallow — so the ball recovers from kicks more slowly, and its jiggling gets bigger and more persistent. Rising variance and rising autocorrelation are the fingerprints of a system losing its grip. Both are computed live in your browser from the real temperature record, using the same statistics behind the recent AMOC warnings (Boers 2021; Ditlevsen & Ditlevsen 2023).

Where this goes as science.

The portrait is the front door. Behind it is a research program: make the dynamical-systems view of climate operational — measured continuously, on open data, for every major tipping element.

A live tipping-point observatory

Early-warning indicators computed continuously from open feeds — RAPID overturning transport, subpolar-gyre SST, GRACE ice mass, Amazon vegetation — published as a versioned, citable data product instead of a one-off paper figure.

Potentials reconstructed from data

The well above is a cartoon; the real one can be estimated. Langevin–Fokker–Planck reconstruction turns observed fluctuations into an empirical landscape — letting us watch the well flatten in measurements, not assumptions.

Coupled tipping cascades

Greenland melt freshens the Atlantic; a slower AMOC shifts monsoons and Amazon rain. A probabilistic cascade graph over the tipping elements turns local warnings into a global risk picture.
